How hot is too hot for a Newfoundland? It's one of the most useful questions a Newfoundland owner can ask, because heat affects this breed in specific ways tied to its build, its coat and its temperament. Here's what actually makes a Newfoundland vulnerable, the temperatures where things get risky, and the habits that keep it safe through the warm months.

## Why the Newfoundland is vulnerable to heat

Dogs cool themselves by panting, but they also have to fight their own insulation. The Newfoundland carries a thick double coat built to trap body heat in cold climates, so on a warm day that coat works against it, holding warmth against the skin while the dog struggles to pant the heat away.

The Newfoundland is a cold-water working giant, with a massive body and a thick, oily, insulating double coat. That coat is built to keep the dog warm in icy water — in a heatwave it works against the dog completely.

## Heat-risk profile: Newfoundland

| Factor | Newfoundland | Effect on heat tolerance |
|---|---|---|
| Face shape | Normal muzzle | Neutral |
| Typical weight | ~60 kg (132 lb) | Heavy body stores and sheds heat slowly |
| Coat | Thick | ⬇️ Traps warmth against the body |
| Overall heat risk | **Very high** | Caution from mild temperatures |

Because a Newfoundland's coat also shields against heat and sunburn, shaving is a mistake — brush out the dead undercoat instead so air can move through it.

## What temperature is dangerous for a Newfoundland?

There's no single magic number — humidity, sun and your individual dog all matter — but for a Newfoundland the practical guide is:

- **From 20 °C (68 °F):** be attentive; keep walks short, shaded and calm.
- **From 24–25 °C (75–77 °F):** the danger zone; walk only in the coolest parts of the day, on grass and in shade, and keep it brief.
- **Above 28 °C (82 °F):** high risk even at rest; keep your Newfoundland indoors with cool air and skip walks beyond quick bathroom breaks.

Humidity makes all of this worse, because muggy air blocks the evaporation that panting depends on. Early-morning and after-dark outings, when the air and the pavement have both cooled, are by far the safest.

## Warning signs to watch in your Newfoundland

Act on the first signal rather than waiting for several:

- **Louder, harder, faster panting** than usual
- **A very red, or bluish/pale, tongue and gums**
- **Thick, ropey drool** and repeated swallowing
- **Slowing down, lagging, or seeking cool floors**
- **Wobbliness, glazed eyes, or refusing to move** — these are emergencies

{{< alerte type="attention" >}}
If your Newfoundland's gums or tongue turn **blue or pale**, or it can't settle and breathe normally, treat it as a **life-threatening emergency**: start cooling immediately and get to a veterinarian without delay.

## Keeping your Newfoundland cool: practical tips

Prevention is the whole game. A few habits make an enormous difference:

- **Shift the schedule** to early morning and after sunset, never the 11 a.m.–6 p.m. window on warm days.
- Never shave the protective coat; brush out the undercoat and keep the dog in cool, air-conditioned comfort.
- Avoid heat and exertion entirely on warm days, and provide constant cool water — or a cool swim.
- **Do the [7-second pavement test](/hot-pavement-dog-paws/)** before walks — if the ground is too hot for your hand, it's too hot for paws.
- **Never leave your dog in a car**, even briefly with the windows cracked; interiors pass 50 °C (122 °F) within minutes.

One of the least heat-tolerant breeds; restrict travel to cool hours and air-conditioned spaces. For planning, see our guides to [flying with a dog](/flying-with-a-dog/) and [dog travel anxiety](/dog-travel-anxiety/).
