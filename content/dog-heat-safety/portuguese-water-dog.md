How hot is too hot for a Portuguese Water Dog? It's one of the most useful questions a Portuguese Water Dog owner can ask, because heat affects this breed in specific ways tied to its build, its coat and its temperament. Here's what actually makes a Portuguese Water Dog vulnerable, the temperatures where things get risky, and the habits that keep it safe through the warm months.

## Why the Portuguese Water Dog is vulnerable to heat

Dogs shed heat mainly by panting, which has limits. The Portuguese Water Dog cools reasonably well for its build, but its real risk is exertion: a driven, active dog will keep working long past the point it should stop to cool down.

The Portuguese Water Dog's curly, non-shedding coat is dense and holds warmth, though the breed's love of water is a real asset. It's an active working dog that will keep going, so exertion in heat is the main risk.

## Heat-risk profile: Portuguese Water Dog

| Factor | Portuguese Water Dog | Effect on heat tolerance |
|---|---|---|
| Face shape | Normal muzzle | Neutral |
| Typical weight | ~22 kg (49 lb) | Keep lean for better tolerance |
| Coat | Curly | Modest help; not the limiting factor |
| Overall heat risk | **Moderate** | Sensible precautions suffice |

Keep the coat groomed and the undercoat clear so it can breathe; a matted coat traps far more heat.

## What temperature is dangerous for a Portuguese Water Dog?

There's no single magic number — humidity, sun and your individual dog all matter — but for a Portuguese Water Dog the practical guide is:

- **From 24 °C (75 °F):** be attentive; keep walks short, shaded and calm.
- **From 27–28 °C (81–82 °F):** the danger zone; walk only in the coolest parts of the day, on grass and in shade, and keep it brief.
- **Above 32 °C (90 °F):** high risk even at rest; keep your Portuguese Water Dog indoors with cool air and skip walks beyond quick bathroom breaks.

Humidity makes all of this worse, because muggy air blocks the evaporation that panting depends on. Early-morning and after-dark outings, when the air and the pavement have both cooled, are by far the safest.

## Warning signs to watch in your Portuguese Water Dog

Act on the first signal rather than waiting for several:

- **Louder, harder, faster panting** than usual
- **A very red, or bluish/pale, tongue and gums**
- **Thick, ropey drool** and repeated swallowing
- **Slowing down, lagging, or seeking cool floors**
- **Wobbliness, glazed eyes, or refusing to move** — these are emergencies

{{< alerte type="attention" >}}
If your Portuguese Water Dog's gums or tongue turn **blue or pale**, or it can't settle and breathe normally, treat it as a **life-threatening emergency**: start cooling immediately and get to a veterinarian without delay.
{{< /alerte >}}

## Keeping your Portuguese Water Dog cool: practical tips

Prevention is the whole game. A few habits make an enormous difference:

- **Shift the schedule** to early morning and after sunset, never the 11 a.m.–6 p.m. window on warm days.
- Let this water-loving breed cool off with a swim, and keep the curly coat trimmed and mat-free in summer.
- Exercise in the cool hours and provide shade and water.
- **Do the [7-second pavement test](/hot-pavement-dog-paws/)** before walks — if the ground is too hot for your hand, it's too hot for paws.
- **Never leave your dog in a car**, even briefly with the windows cracked; interiors pass 50 °C (122 °F) within minutes.
