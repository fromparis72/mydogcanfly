How hot is too hot for a Bichon Frise? It's one of the most useful questions a Bichon Frise owner can ask, because heat affects this breed in specific ways tied to its build, its coat and its temperament. Here's what actually makes a Bichon Frise vulnerable, the temperatures where things get risky, and the habits that keep it safe through the warm months.

## Why the Bichon Frise is vulnerable to heat

Small dogs have very little thermal reserve, and the Bichon Frise compounds that with a dense, warm coat. Its tiny body heats up quickly, and the coat holds that warmth close to the skin.

The Bichon's soft, curly double coat is dense and holds warmth, even though the dog itself is small. A little body has limited heat reserve, so a Bichon can warm up quickly on a hot day.

| Factor | Bichon Frise | Effect on heat tolerance |
|---|---|---|
| Face shape | Normal muzzle | Neutral |
| Typical weight | ~6 kg (13 lb) | Compact, but excess weight adds risk |
| Coat | Curly | ⬇️ Traps warmth against the body |
| Overall heat risk | **Moderate** | Sensible precautions suffice |

Because a Bichon Frise's coat also shields against heat and sunburn, shaving is a mistake — brush out the dead undercoat instead so air can move through it.

## What temperature is dangerous for a Bichon Frise?

There's no single magic number — humidity, sun and your individual dog all matter — but for a Bichon Frise the practical guide is:

- **From 24 °C (75 °F):** be attentive; keep walks short, shaded and calm.
- **From 27–28 °C (81–82 °F):** the danger zone; walk only in the coolest parts of the day, on grass and in shade, and keep it brief.
- **Above 32 °C (90 °F):** high risk even at rest; keep your Bichon Frise indoors with cool air and skip walks beyond quick bathroom breaks.

Humidity makes all of this worse, because muggy air blocks the evaporation that panting depends on. Early-morning and after-dark outings, when the air and the pavement have both cooled, are by far the safest.

## Warning signs to watch in your Bichon Frise

Act on the first signal rather than waiting for several:

- **Louder, harder, faster panting** than usual
- **A very red, or bluish/pale, tongue and gums**
- **Thick, ropey drool** and repeated swallowing
- **Slowing down, lagging, or seeking cool floors**
- **Wobbliness, glazed eyes, or refusing to move** — these are emergencies

{{< alerte type="attention" >}}
If your Bichon Frise's gums or tongue turn **blue or pale**, or it can't settle and breathe normally, treat it as a **life-threatening emergency**: start cooling immediately and get to a veterinarian without delay.

## Keeping your Bichon Frise cool: practical tips

Prevention is the whole game. A few habits make an enormous difference:

- **Shift the schedule** to early morning and after sunset, never the 11 a.m.–6 p.m. window on warm days.
- A neat summer trim helps this dense-coated breed, and cool, shaded walks suit it best.
- Keep the coat brushed out — matting traps heat and moisture.
- **Do the [7-second pavement test](/hot-pavement-dog-paws/)** before walks — if the ground is too hot for your hand, it's too hot for paws.
- **Never leave your dog in a car**, even briefly with the windows cracked; interiors pass 50 °C (122 °F) within minutes.
