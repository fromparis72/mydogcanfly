How hot is too hot for a Bernese Mountain Dog? It's one of the most useful questions a Bernese Mountain Dog owner can ask, because heat affects this breed in specific ways tied to its build, its coat and its temperament. Here's what actually makes a Bernese Mountain Dog vulnerable, the temperatures where things get risky, and the habits that keep it safe through the warm months.

## Why the Bernese Mountain Dog is vulnerable to heat

Dogs cool themselves by panting, but they also have to fight their own insulation. The Bernese Mountain Dog carries a thick double coat built to trap body heat in cold climates, so on a warm day that coat works against it, holding warmth against the skin while the dog struggles to pant the heat away.

The Bernese was bred for cold Alpine farms, and its long, thick, black-and-tan coat is built to hold in warmth. A giant body plus a heavy dark coat makes this one of the least heat-tolerant popular breeds.

## Heat-risk profile: Bernese Mountain Dog

| Factor | Bernese Mountain Dog | Effect on heat tolerance |
|---|---|---|
| Face shape | Normal muzzle | Neutral |
| Typical weight | ~45 kg (99 lb) | Heavy body stores and sheds heat slowly |
| Coat | Thick | ⬇️ Traps warmth against the body |
| Overall heat risk | **Very high** | Caution from mild temperatures |

Because a Bernese Mountain Dog's coat also shields against heat and sunburn, shaving is a mistake — brush out the dead undercoat instead so air can move through it.

## What temperature is dangerous for a Bernese Mountain Dog?

There's no single magic number — humidity, sun and your individual dog all matter — but for a Bernese Mountain Dog the practical guide is:

- **From 20 °C (68 °F):** be attentive; keep walks short, shaded and calm.
- **From 24–25 °C (75–77 °F):** the danger zone; walk only in the coolest parts of the day, on grass and in shade, and keep it brief.
- **Above 28 °C (82 °F):** high risk even at rest; keep your Bernese Mountain Dog indoors with cool air and skip walks beyond quick bathroom breaks.

Humidity makes all of this worse, because muggy air blocks the evaporation that panting depends on. Early-morning and after-dark outings, when the air and the pavement have both cooled, are by far the safest.

## Warning signs to watch in your Bernese Mountain Dog

Act on the first signal rather than waiting for several:

- **Louder, harder, faster panting** than usual
- **A very red, or bluish/pale, tongue and gums**
- **Thick, ropey drool** and repeated swallowing
- **Slowing down, lagging, or seeking cool floors**
- **Wobbliness, glazed eyes, or refusing to move** — these are emergencies

{{< alerte type="attention" >}}
If your Bernese Mountain Dog's gums or tongue turn **blue or pale**, or it can't settle and breathe normally, treat it as a **life-threatening emergency**: start cooling immediately and get to a veterinarian without delay.

## Keeping your Bernese Mountain Dog cool: practical tips

Prevention is the whole game. A few habits make an enormous difference:

- **Shift the schedule** to early morning and after sunset, never the 11 a.m.–6 p.m. window on warm days.
- Never shave the coat — brush out the undercoat and keep the dog in cool, shaded, air-conditioned comfort.
- Avoid all but the gentlest activity in the heat, and provide constant cool water.
- **Do the [7-second pavement test](/hot-pavement-dog-paws/)** before walks — if the ground is too hot for your hand, it's too hot for paws.
- **Never leave your dog in a car**, even briefly with the windows cracked; interiors pass 50 °C (122 °F) within minutes.
