How hot is too hot for a Pomeranian? It's one of the most useful questions a Pomeranian owner can ask, because heat affects this breed in specific ways tied to its build, its coat and its temperament. Here's what actually makes a Pomeranian vulnerable, the temperatures where things get risky, and the habits that keep it safe through the warm months.

## Why the Pomeranian is vulnerable to heat

Small dogs have very little thermal reserve, and the Pomeranian compounds that with a dense, warm coat. Its tiny body heats up quickly, and the coat holds that warmth close to the skin.

The Pomeranian's tiny body is wrapped in a thick, stand-off double coat that holds heat close to the skin. A small dog has little thermal reserve, so a Pom can heat up fast on a warm day.

Because a Pomeranian's coat also shields against heat and sunburn, shaving is a mistake — brush out the dead undercoat instead so air can move through it.

## What temperature is dangerous for a Pomeranian?

There's no single magic number — humidity, sun and your individual dog all matter — but for a Pomeranian the practical guide is:

- **From 22 °C (72 °F):** be attentive; keep walks short, shaded and calm.
- **From 25–26 °C (77–79 °F):** the danger zone; walk only in the coolest parts of the day, on grass and in shade, and keep it brief.
- **Above 30 °C (86 °F):** high risk even at rest; keep your Pomeranian indoors with cool air and skip walks beyond quick bathroom breaks.

Humidity makes all of this worse, because muggy air blocks the evaporation that panting depends on. Early-morning and after-dark outings, when the air and the pavement have both cooled, are by far the safest.

## Warning signs to watch in your Pomeranian

Act on the first signal rather than waiting for several:

- **Louder, harder, faster panting** than usual
- **A very red, or bluish/pale, tongue and gums**
- **Thick, ropey drool** and repeated swallowing
- **Slowing down, lagging, or seeking cool floors**
- **Wobbliness, glazed eyes, or refusing to move** — these are emergencies

{{< alerte type="attention" >}}
If your Pomeranian's gums or tongue turn **blue or pale**, or it can't settle and breathe normally, treat it as a **life-threatening emergency**: start cooling immediately and get to a veterinarian without delay.

## Keeping your Pomeranian cool: practical tips

Prevention is the whole game. A few habits make an enormous difference:

- **Shift the schedule** to early morning and after sunset, never the 11 a.m.–6 p.m. window on warm days.
- Brush out the dense undercoat rather than shaving it, and keep the Pom in cool, shaded, airy spaces.
- Carry water on outings and keep walks short in summer.
- **Do the [7-second pavement test](/hot-pavement-dog-paws/)** before walks — if the ground is too hot for your hand, it's too hot for paws.
- **Never leave your dog in a car**, even briefly with the windows cracked; interiors pass 50 °C (122 °F) within minutes.

## Traveling with a Pomeranian in the heat

Poms travel easily in a carrier — just keep it out of direct sun and in a cool, ventilated cabin. For planning, see our guides to [flying with a dog](/flying-with-a-dog/) and [dog travel anxiety](/dog-travel-anxiety/).
