How hot is too hot for a Alaskan Malamute? It's one of the most useful questions a Alaskan Malamute owner can ask, because heat affects this breed in specific ways tied to its build, its coat and its temperament. ## Why the Alaskan Malamute is vulnerable to heat

Dogs cool themselves by panting, but they also have to fight their own insulation. The Alaskan Malamute carries a thick double coat built to trap body heat in cold climates, so on a warm day that coat works against it, holding warmth against the skin while the dog struggles to pant the heat away.

The Alaskan Malamute is a heavy Arctic sled dog with one of the thickest, warmest double coats of any breed. It was engineered to haul freight in sub-zero cold — a hot climate is the opposite of what its body is built for.

## Heat-risk profile: Alaskan Malamute

| Factor | Alaskan Malamute | Effect on heat tolerance |
|---|---|---|
| Face shape | Normal muzzle | Neutral |
| Typical weight | ~38 kg (84 lb) | Heavy body stores and sheds heat slowly |
| Coat | Thick | ⬇️ Traps warmth against the body |
| Overall heat risk | **Very high** | Caution from mild temperatures |

Because a Alaskan Malamute's coat also shields against heat and sunburn, shaving is a mistake — brush out the dead undercoat instead so air can move through it.

## What temperature is dangerous for a Alaskan Malamute?

There's no single magic number — humidity, sun and your individual dog all matter — but for a Alaskan Malamute the practical guide is:

- **From 20 °C (68 °F):** be attentive; keep walks short, shaded and calm.
- **From 24–25 °C (75–77 °F):** the danger zone; walk only in the coolest parts of the day, on grass and in shade, and keep it brief.
- **Above 28 °C (82 °F):** high risk even at rest; keep your Alaskan Malamute indoors with cool air and skip walks beyond quick bathroom breaks.

Humidity makes all of this worse, because muggy air blocks the evaporation that panting depends on. Early-morning and after-dark outings, when the air and the pavement have both cooled, are by far the safest.

## Warning signs to watch in your Alaskan Malamute

Act on the first signal rather than waiting for several:

- **Louder, harder, faster panting** than usual
- **A very red, or bluish/pale, tongue and gums**
- **Thick, ropey drool** and repeated swallowing
- **Slowing down, lagging, or seeking cool floors**
- **Wobbliness, glazed eyes, or refusing to move** — these are emergencies

If your Alaskan Malamute's gums or tongue turn **blue or pale**, or it can't settle and breathe normally, treat it as a **life-threatening emergency**: start cooling immediately and get to a veterinarian without delay.

## Keeping your Alaskan Malamute cool: practical tips

Prevention is the whole game. A few habits make an enormous difference:

- **Shift the schedule** to early morning and after sunset, never the 11 a.m.–6 p.m. window on warm days.
- Never shave the coat; blow out the undercoat as it sheds and keep the dog in cool, air-conditioned comfort.
- Restrict activity to the coolest hours and provide abundant cool water and cool surfaces.
- **Do the [7-second pavement test](/hot-pavement-dog-paws/)** before walks — if the ground is too hot for your hand, it's too hot for paws.
- **Never leave your dog in a car**, even briefly with the windows cracked; interiors pass 50 °C (122 °F) within minutes.

Among the least heat-tolerant of all breeds — keep travel to cool hours and air-conditioned spaces. For planning, see our guides to [flying with a dog](/flying-with-a-dog/) and [dog travel anxiety](/dog-travel-anxiety/).
