## Why the Shetland Sheepdog is vulnerable to heat

Small dogs have very little thermal reserve, and the Shetland Sheepdog compounds that with a dense, warm coat. Its tiny body heats up quickly, and the coat holds that warmth close to the skin.

The Sheltie's long, dense double coat is built for cold Scottish islands and holds warmth against the body. It's a busy, herding-minded dog that stays active and vocal even when it's too warm to.

Because a Shetland Sheepdog's coat also shields against heat and sunburn, shaving is a mistake — brush out the dead undercoat instead so air can move through it.

## What temperature is dangerous for a Shetland Sheepdog?

There's no single magic number — humidity, sun and your individual dog all matter — but for a Shetland Sheepdog the practical guide is:

- **From 22 °C (72 °F):** be attentive; keep walks short, shaded and calm.
- **From 25–26 °C (77–79 °F):** the danger zone; walk only in the coolest parts of the day, on grass and in shade, and keep it brief.
- **Above 30 °C (86 °F):** high risk even at rest; keep your Shetland Sheepdog indoors with cool air and skip walks beyond quick bathroom breaks.

Humidity makes all of this worse, because muggy air blocks the evaporation that panting depends on. Early-morning and after-dark outings, when the air and the pavement have both cooled, are by far the safest.

## Warning signs to watch in your Shetland Sheepdog

Act on the first signal rather than waiting for several:

- **Louder, harder, faster panting** than usual
- **A very red, or bluish/pale, tongue and gums**
- **Thick, ropey drool** and repeated swallowing
- **Slowing down, lagging, or seeking cool floors**
- **Wobbliness, glazed eyes, or refusing to move** — these are emergencies

If your Shetland Sheepdog's gums or tongue turn **blue or pale**, or it can't settle and breathe normally, treat it as a **life-threatening emergency**: start cooling immediately and get to a veterinarian without delay.

## Keeping your Shetland Sheepdog cool: practical tips

Prevention is the whole game. A few habits make an enormous difference:

- **Shift the schedule** to early morning and after sunset, never the 11 a.m.–6 p.m. window on warm days.
- Brush out the heavy undercoat rather than shaving, and exercise in the cool hours.
- Offer shade, water and a cool resting spot on hot days.
- **Do the [7-second pavement test](/hot-pavement-dog-paws/)** before walks — if the ground is too hot for your hand, it's too hot for paws.
- **Never leave your dog in a car**, even briefly with the windows cracked; interiors pass 50 °C (122 °F) within minutes.
