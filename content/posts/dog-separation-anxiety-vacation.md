On vacation, a dog loses their bearings: new lodging, new noises, a different rhythm. A dog prone to **separation anxiety** may struggle with the moments when they're left alone. A little preparation goes a long way toward limiting the stress.

*This article is for information only and does not replace advice from a veterinarian.*

## Recognizing separation anxiety

It shows up when the dog is left alone: **barking or howling**, **destruction** (especially near exits), unusual **house soiling**, drooling, restlessness. On vacation, an unfamiliar environment can amplify these reactions, even in a dog who is usually relaxed.

## Recreate familiar cues

Bring their **usual bed** (unwashed, to keep the scent), a **familiar toy**, and stick to their **routines** as much as possible (meal times, walk times). These anchors help them feel safe in a new place.

## Build up alone time

In the vacation rental, **gradually get them used** to staying alone: a few minutes at first, then longer, leaving them something to do (a treat-dispensing toy). Keep **departures and returns low-key** so the separation isn't a big event. A dog tired out by a good walk settles down much more easily.

{{< alerte type="verifier" >}}
If the anxiety is **severe or long-standing** (panic, major destruction, self-injury), talk to your **veterinarian** before you leave: behavioral support, or other appropriate help, may be an option.
