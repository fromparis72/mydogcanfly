A ferry crossing is a practical way to reach an island or another country without being separated from your dog and without the constraints of flying. But every operator has its own rules for pets, and improvising on departure day can quickly turn into a headache. This article is for owners preparing a crossing — short hop or overnight — who want to know where their dog will travel, how to book, and how to limit stress and seasickness. You'll leave with a clear method for boarding with confidence.

## Where does a dog travel on a ferry?

It all depends on the operator and the length of the crossing, but your dog generally travels in one of three spaces. Always check the operator's exact policy when you book.

The most common setups are:

- **The pet-friendly cabin**, where your dog stays with you: the most reassuring option, often limited in number.
- **The onboard kennel**, a dedicated area where animals are housed in crates, sometimes with supervised visits allowed.
- **Your vehicle on the car deck**, permitted by some operators on short crossings, but to be avoided in hot weather.
- **Outdoor decks or designated areas**, where a leashed dog can sometimes stay with you.

Since the drive is often the leg right before the crossing, our guide to [traveling by car with a dog](/traveling-by-car-with-a-dog/) will help you prepare the road portion of the trip.

## How do you book your dog's spot on a ferry?

The golden rule is to book your dog's spot at the same time as yours, and as early as possible. Pet-friendly cabins and onboard kennels are limited and sell out fast in high season.

For a hitch-free booking:

1. Choose the operator and the crossing, then check its **pet policy** before paying.
2. Declare your dog at **booking** and pay any pet fee.
3. Specify the **type of accommodation you want** (cabin, kennel, vehicle) based on the available options.
4. Confirm the **documents required** at boarding and on arrival.
5. Arrive at the port **early** to handle the pet check-in calmly.

## Which documents and health rules apply?

The requirements depend on the destination, not the ferry itself: an international crossing involves the same formalities as any other mode of transportation. Your dog must be identified and, depending on the country, have the required travel document (an EU pet passport or health certificate, depending on your route) and a current rabies vaccination.

Before boarding, check:

- **Microchip identification** and the **travel document** if you're crossing a border.
- A valid **rabies vaccination** for destinations that require it.
- **Parasite treatments** required by some island destinations.
- The **operator's rules** on leashes, a possible muzzle requirement and access to shared spaces.

Start these steps several weeks in advance, because some vaccinations are only valid after a regulatory waiting period.

## How do you limit seasickness and stress?

Seasickness affects dogs too, and it's best prevented with good preparation and a light stomach. Repeated yawning, drooling, restlessness or vomiting are the signs to watch for during the crossing.

For a smoother crossing:

- Give a **light meal** several hours before boarding — never right before.
- Offer a **potty break** before getting on board.
- Keep **water, a collapsible bowl, a blanket and a familiar toy** within reach.
- Stay **calm and present**: your dog picks up on your emotional state.

If your dog is particularly prone to apprehension, our guide to [dog travel anxiety](/dog-travel-anxiety/) offers concrete ways to help them handle the trip more calmly.

## What should you bring for comfort during the crossing?

Comfort mostly comes down to water, staying cool and letting your dog rest somewhere quiet. On long or overnight crossings, a cabin shared with you often makes all the difference.

Remember to pack:

- **Fresh water** and a bowl — sea air and stress make dogs thirsty.
- Your dog's **usual blanket** and a toy for reassurance.
- **Waste bags** and something to clean up in case of an accident.
- Their **leash and harness** for moving around the boat in permitted areas.

Avoid leaving your dog alone and stressed in a closed vehicle, especially in hot weather, when the temperature can climb dangerously fast.
