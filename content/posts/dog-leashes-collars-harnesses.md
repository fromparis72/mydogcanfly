When you travel, the leash-collar-harness trio is your safety link to your dog in an unfamiliar environment. Good gear prevents escapes and keeps walks comfortable for both of you.

## Collar or harness: which should you choose?

The **collar** works for carrying the ID tag and for dogs that walk nicely on a leash, but it puts pressure on the neck whenever the dog pulls. The **harness** spreads the force across the chest: it's safer and more comfortable for travel, especially for dogs that pull, breeds with fragile tracheas, or brachycephalic (short-muzzled) dogs. Many owners keep the collar (for the tag) **and** a harness for walks.

## Fitting the harness properly

A poorly adjusted harness injures the dog or lets them slip out. Adjust each strap so you can slide **two fingers** underneath — no more. Check that it doesn't rub under the armpits and that the dog can't back out of it. Remember to readjust it after a bath or a grooming trim.

## Which leash for travel?

A **leash of about 1.5 m (5 ft)** in nylon or biothane (water-resistant, easy to clean) is the best compromise. Add a **long line** of 5 to 10 m (16 to 33 ft) to give your dog some freedom in areas where it's allowed. Avoid retractable leashes in cities and near roads: they offer little control.
