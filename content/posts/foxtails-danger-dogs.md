In summer, in dry grass, **foxtails** (barbed grass awns) are a real danger: their pointed, arrow-like shape lets them **work their way under the skin** or into a body opening, causing abscesses and infections. Prevention and post-walk checks are essential.

*This article is for information only and does not replace advice from a veterinarian.*

## Why foxtails are dangerous

Their barbed structure only lets them move in one direction: once caught in the coat and then embedded, a foxtail **keeps burrowing** and never comes out on its own. It can migrate under the skin, into the ear canal, under an eyelid, into a nostril or between the toes, causing pain, abscesses and infection.

## Warning signs

Depending on where it lodges: **limping** and licking of one paw (between the toes), **head shaking** and ear scratching, **violent, repeated sneezing** (nose), **a closed, watery eye** (eyelid). These signs, especially after a walk through grass, should put you on alert.

## Prevent and inspect

After every summer outing through dry grass, **systematically check** the paws (between the pads), the ears, around the eyes and the coat. **Trim** the hair between the pads of long-haired dogs and brush them out. Remove a foxtail that is visible and superficial; if it's embedded, or at any warning sign, **see a vet without delay**.

{{< alerte type="attention" >}}
An embedded foxtail does not come out on its own and **gets worse** over time. If your dog is limping, sneezing violently, has a painful ear or a closed eye after a walk, see a veterinarian **quickly**.
