Just like heat, **cold** carries risks, especially for certain dogs. In the mountains, in winter or after a swim in cold water, a dog can chill dangerously fast. Knowing how to spot the signs prevents hypothermia.

*This article is for informational purposes and does not replace advice from a veterinarian.*

## Which dogs are most sensitive?

**Small dogs**, dogs with a **short coat** or no undercoat, **puppies**, **senior**, thin or sick dogs cool down faster. Nordic breeds (husky, malamute) tolerate cold better. Dampness and wind make the cold feel worse for every dog.

## Recognizing the signs

A cold dog **shivers**, curls up into a ball, becomes **lethargic**, has **cold extremities** (ears, paws) and a stiff gait. With more marked hypothermia: weakness, slowed breathing and heart rate, disorientation — that's an **emergency**.

## Warming up and prevention

Bring the dog inside, **dry them off** if they're wet, wrap them in a **blanket** and settle them in a mild room: warming must be **gradual**. For prevention, a **coat** helps sensitive dogs, limit time outdoors in severe cold, and avoid swims in icy water. Also protect the paw pads from de-icing road salt.

{{< alerte type="attention" >}}
**Hypothermia** (a very weak, disoriented dog with slowed breathing) is a **life-threatening emergency**: warm gradually and get to a veterinarian without delay. Never rewarm abruptly (no direct heat source).
{{< /alerte >}}
