Traveling with your dog isn't just about packing one more bag. The right gear turns a stressful trip into a smooth one — for them and for you. But between marketing claims and real needs, it's hard to sort things out. This guide organizes everything by category of use, with a link to our **detailed buying guide** for each topic (criteria, comparisons, veterinary advice). The goal: that you know exactly **what to bring, why, and how to choose it**.

## How do you keep your dog safe during the ride?

This is priority number one — and in many places a legal requirement: in a car, an unrestrained dog becomes a projectile under hard braking, and road rules in most countries require that an animal not interfere with the driver. Two reliable solutions: a **safety harness clipped to the seat belt** (for dogs used to it) or a properly **strapped-down travel crate**. Avoid attaching a plain collar to the seat belt — it's dangerous for the neck.

To choose, read our guide on [which car travel gear is actually useful](/car-travel-dog-gear/) and the one dedicated to [crash-tested car harnesses](/dog-car-harness/). And if your dog pulls on the leash day to day, the right model isn't necessarily the same: see [which harness to choose for a dog that pulls](/no-pull-dog-harness/).

## Which crate or carrier should you choose?

The container depends on your dog's size and your mode of transport. For a **small dog**, a ventilated soft carrier is often enough and is accepted in the cabin on planes and trains — see [the most comfortable carriers for small dogs](/small-dog-carriers/). For a **medium to large dog**, or for air travel in the hold, you need a properly sized, **IATA-compliant rigid crate**.

Three resources to get it right: the [carrier vs. crate comparison](/dog-carriers-and-crates/), our guide on [choosing an airline-approved crate](/airline-approved-dog-crate/), and the complete article on [flying with a dog](/flying-with-a-dog/) for the paperwork.

## How do you keep your dog hydrated and fed on the road?

Dogs dehydrate quickly while traveling, especially in summer. The ideal setup: a **dog water bottle** or a **collapsible bowl** that slips into your bag, plus a **water fountain** at home so they drink more before departure. For meals, stick to the same food as at home to avoid digestive upsets.

Our guides: [the best travel dog bowls](/best-travel-dog-bowls/), [the best dog water fountains](/best-dog-water-fountains-2026/), the overview of [portable feeding gear](/portable-dog-feeding-gear/) and the right habits for [feeding and hydrating your dog while traveling](/feeding-your-dog-while-traveling/).

## How do you avoid losing your dog far from home?

A runaway or lost dog is the number one fear on vacation, in unfamiliar surroundings. A microchip is mandatory in many countries but it **cannot locate** your dog: to follow them in real time, a **GPS tracker** attached to the collar is the accessory that changes everything, especially when hiking or camping.

To understand battery life, subscriptions and range, read [GPS trackers for dogs: never lose them again](/dog-gps-trackers/).

## Which comfort accessories for which weather?

Travel exposes your dog to heat and cold alike. In summer, a **cooling mat** helps them regulate their temperature during breaks, and protecting their **paw pads** is essential on scorching pavement. In winter or in the mountains, a **coat** for cold-sensitive builds and **booties** protect against cold, road salt and snow.

Worth reading: [the best dog cooling mats](/best-dog-cooling-mats/), [how to protect paw pads in summer](/hot-pavement-dog-paws/), [protecting paw pads year-round](/protecting-dog-paw-pads/) and [dog coats and boots for every forecast](/dog-coats-and-boots/). In hot weather, keep the warning signs of [heatstroke](/dog-heatstroke/) in mind.

## How do you protect your dog's health and keep them calm while traveling?

Two health essentials: something to **soothe** a dog stressed by travel (calming sprays, collars or supplements, on your vet's advice) and a well-stocked **first-aid kit**. Motion sickness and anxiety can also be improved through gradual habituation.

Our guides: [dog travel anxiety](/dog-travel-anxiety/), [motion sickness in dogs](/dog-motion-sickness/) and [the canine first-aid kit](/dog-first-aid-kit/).

## The dog travel accessories checklist

To leave with peace of mind, make sure you have:

- a **secure transport system**: a crash-tested car harness or a strapped-down crate;
- an **approved crate/carrier** if flying, or taking the train or a ferry;
- a **water bottle + collapsible bowl** and your dog's usual food;
- a **GPS tracker** and an ID tag with your contact details;
- **weather gear**: cooling mat in summer, coat/booties in winter;
- a **first-aid kit** + up-to-date parasite prevention;
- the **paperwork**: health record, pet passport or health certificate, and vaccination proof if going abroad.
