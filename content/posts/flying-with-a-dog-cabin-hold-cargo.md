Flying with a dog is not a one-answer question. Depending on the animal's size, the airline, the destination and your situation, several solutions exist — and they don't come with the same price, the same comfort level or the same constraints. Here is the complete panorama of ways to take a dog into the air, from the most common to the most exotic. To find the right airline for your route, keep our ["can my dog fly?" checker](/tools/can-my-dog-fly/) and the [comparison of all 62 airlines](/airline-pet-policies/) close at hand.

## 1. In the cabin: the most reassuring option

The cabin is reserved for **small dogs**. The near-universal rule: the dog **and** its travel carrier must not exceed a maximum weight, most often **8 kg (18 lb)** (sometimes 6 kg / 13 lb, up to 10 kg / 22 lb at a few low-cost carriers such as Vueling or Volotea). The carrier must be **soft-sided**, within the airline's required dimensions, and fit **under the seat** in front of you. The animal must stay inside for the entire flight.

It's the most reassuring formula: your dog stays with you, at normal temperature, far from the stress of the hold. The fee generally ranges from **€30 to €250 (~$35–290)** depending on distance. Since the number of pet spots in the cabin is limited (often 1 to 6 per aircraft), book early and by phone. To prepare your companion for the flight, see our advice on [dog travel anxiety](/dog-travel-anxiety/).

## 2. In the hold: for bigger dogs

Above the cabin weight limit, the dog travels in the **hold**, in a **pressurized, heated compartment** designed for live animals (code AVIH in airline jargon: *Animal in Hold*). This is always **accompanied baggage**: your dog travels on **the same flight as you**, under your ticket.

The conditions: a **rigid IATA-approved crate** (sturdy, ventilated on several sides, with a leak-proof floor and metal hardware), a maximum combined weight for dog + crate (often up to 45 or 75 kg / 99 or 165 lb depending on the airline), and an advance reservation. Crucial point: most airlines **ban brachycephalic breeds from the hold** (bulldogs, pugs, boxers…) for respiratory health reasons. Size the crate with our [IATA crate calculator](/tools/iata-dog-crate-calculator/) and read our guide to [choosing an airline-approved crate](/airline-approved-dog-crate/).

## 3. As cargo: not to be confused with the hold

This is the distinction many people miss. **Cargo** (freight) is **not** the same thing as the hold as accompanied baggage. As cargo, the dog travels as **merchandise shipped by the airline's cargo division**, possibly **on a different flight from yours**, with its own logistics.

It's used in several cases: a dog **too heavy** for the passenger hold (beyond ~45–75 kg / 99–165 lb with the crate), an airline that has **dropped hold transport for passengers** (the case of Delta, United and American Airlines in the US, which now only offer cargo), or destinations that only accept animals **via freight** (the UK, or the UAE for Dubai…). Cargo transport is **priced by quote**, often through a specialized agent (a "pet shipper"), with heavier customs paperwork. It's the most expensive and most bureaucratic option, but sometimes the only one possible for a large dog on a long-haul flight.

## 4. The service dog: the cabin, for free

**Properly trained service dogs** — first and foremost **guide dogs for blind or visually impaired people**, but also assistance dogs for mobility impairments, epilepsy, diabetes, and so on — enjoy a status of their own. They are admitted **into the cabin, free of charge**, without a crate, lying at their handler's feet, **whatever their size**.

The conditions concern **proof of status**: the animal must be a certified service dog (in the United States, the airline requires the official DOT form attesting to training and behavior; in Europe, proof of assistance-dog status is requested). The dog must be **house-trained, calm and under control**. This framework is protected by accessibility regulations: an airline cannot refuse a genuine guide dog.

## 5. The emotional support dog: a case that has become rare

Don't confuse a service dog with an **emotional support animal** (ESA). The latter is **not trained** for specific tasks; it soothes by its presence alone. Since a reform by the **US Department of Transportation (DOT) that took effect in early 2021**, airlines are **no longer required to treat emotional support animals as service dogs**. As a result, nearly all major US carriers have **stopped accepting them free in the cabin**; the animal then travels **as an ordinary pet** (in a carrier under the seat, for a fee, and only if it's small enough).

A few carriers in the Americas still recognize ESA status (examples often cited include **LATAM**, **Volaris** and **WestJet**), but always under conditions. In **Europe, emotional support is not a category recognized** by aviation regulations: the dog is treated as a regular pet. In short: don't count on this status to fly a large dog in the cabin for free — always check the airline's exact policy.

## 6. Dog-only airlines: traveling side by side

This is the newest and most spectacular solution. Airlines **entirely dedicated to dogs** have appeared: the dog travels neither in the hold nor in a crate, but **in the cabin, uncrated, next to its human**, aboard **shared private jets** or aircraft fitted out in a premium configuration.

The most publicized is **Bark Air**, launched in May 2024: a charter operator, it flies jets (Gulfstream) and larger aircraft (CRJ-200s that can carry about twenty dogs and their owners), with treat stations, calming pheromones, bone broth as a beverage and paw-pad balms. Fares match the concept: on the order of **$6,000** for New York–Los Angeles and **$8,000** for New York–London, with routes to Paris, Lisbon, Madrid, Berlin and Milan. Another player, **K9 Jets**, based in the UK, offers private flights (for example New York–Paris or Lisbon–London) where the animal travels in the cabin at your side. These options remain a luxury, but they answer a real demand: traveling with a large dog **without ever putting it in the hold**.

## Which option should you choose?

For the vast majority of travelers, the choice comes down to **cabin** (small dog) or **hold** (medium to large dog) on a regular airline — cargo being reserved for special cases, and dog-only airlines for exceptional budgets. Before booking, always check the **destination country's entry requirements** (see our [dog travel requirements by country](/dog-travel-requirements-by-country/)) and compare conditions with the [airline pet policy comparison](/airline-pet-policies/).
