A walk in the woods, on a mountain trail or through the fields sometimes brings bad surprises: a shard of glass, a foxtail lodged between the paw pads, a fall. Knowing how to respond calmly to your dog's minor injuries keeps a small wound from getting worse and eases their pain until you can reach a clinic. This article covers the most common situations on walks and the first aid steps to take, along with when a veterinarian's opinion becomes essential.

*This article is for information only and does not replace advice from a veterinarian.*

## What should I do first when my dog gets hurt on a walk?

Above all, stay calm and make the situation safe for both you and your dog. An injured dog is scared and may react unpredictably, even toward their owner: approach slowly and speak in a steady voice. If necessary — and only if you can do it without stressing them — an improvised muzzle can prevent a reflex bite while you treat the wound.

Then proceed step by step:

1. Move your dog away from the source of danger and settle them in a safe spot.
2. Calmly assess the injury: location, bleeding, presence of a foreign object.
3. Give the first aid the situation calls for, using the contents of your kit.
4. Decide how urgent it is and contact a veterinarian if needed.

A well-stocked [canine first aid kit](/dog-first-aid-kit/) makes all the difference: gauze pads, antiseptic, a bandage, tweezers and a tick remover let you act effectively in the field.

## How do I treat a cut or a paw pad wound?

A cut gets cleaned, disinfected and protected — but a deep or heavily bleeding wound needs a veterinarian. Paw pads, especially exposed on rocky terrain, are prone to cuts that bleed readily and heal slowly because of the constant pressure on them.

For a superficial wound:

- Clean the wound with clear water or saline solution to remove dirt and debris.
- Disinfect with a pet-safe antiseptic.
- Protect with a sterile gauze pad and a bandage that isn't too tight, so you don't cut off circulation.
- Keep your dog from licking the area.

If bleeding is heavy, apply firm pressure with a clean gauze pad and head to a veterinarian. A deep, gaping or dirty wound may need stitches and antibiotic cover: don't put off the visit.

## How do I handle a foxtail?

A foxtail — that small, dart-shaped dry grass seed — can burrow into the skin, between the toes, into an ear or an eye, and migrate dangerously. If you spot a visible, superficial foxtail, for example between the paw pads, you can gently try to remove it with tweezers. But never force it, and never probe inside a body cavity.

The telltale signs of a foxtail are:

- Persistent licking or nibbling at one precise spot, often a paw.
- Repeated head shaking or sudden ear scratching.
- Violent, repeated sneezing, or a watery eye that stays shut.
- A lump or a small oozing hole in the skin.

If the foxtail is embedded, invisible, or located in an ear, an eye or the nose, see a veterinarian quickly: left in place, it can migrate and cause an abscess. These problems are common in summer, particularly on high-altitude outings — see our guide to [mountain vacations with your dog](/mountain-vacations-with-a-dog/).

## What if my dog is limping after a walk?

A limp calls first for rest and a careful examination of the affected leg. Have your dog sit and gently inspect the limb: paw pads, the spaces between the toes, the nails, then work your way up to the joint. You're looking for a thorn, a foreign object, a cut, a broken nail or a spot that's painful to the touch.

Depending on what you find:

- If there's a superficial, accessible foreign object, remove it gently and disinfect.
- If there's mild discomfort with no wound or swelling, rest your dog and monitor how it evolves.
- If there's swelling, sharp pain, a dangling leg or a limp that persists, see a veterinarian.

Never give human pain medication: many anti-inflammatories that are common for people are toxic to dogs. If the limp doesn't improve quickly or gets worse, a veterinary exam is needed to rule out a sprain, a fracture or a more serious injury.

## When is it a veterinary emergency?

Some situations allow no delay and require getting to a veterinarian immediately. In those cases, first aid only serves to stabilize your dog for the ride.

Seek emergency care in case of:

- Heavy bleeding that doesn't stop despite pressure.
- A deep, gaping wound, or one exposing tissue.
- Intense pain, a leg that can't bear weight, suspected fracture.
- A foreign object in an eye, an ear, or one that's hard to remove.
- Breathing difficulties, marked lethargy or shock.

Before setting out on a walk, look up the contact details of a veterinarian and an emergency clinic near where you're staying. When traveling, this habit will save you precious time the day you need it.
