A trip is also prepared on the health front. Depending on the destination, certain treatments come with **non-negotiable waiting periods** (rabies vaccine, rabies titer test): it's best to see your veterinarian early to set the right timeline.

*This article is for information purposes and does not replace the advice of a veterinarian.*

## The vaccines to check

The **rabies vaccine** is required for virtually all international travel and only becomes valid after a waiting period (21 days after primary vaccination, under EU rules and in many other countries). Core vaccines (distemper, hepatitis, parvovirus, parainfluenza, leptospirosis) should be brought up to date based on your dog's lifestyle and the destination. For some regions, vaccination against other diseases may be advisable: ask your veterinarian.

## Deworming and internal parasites

A **recent deworming** is recommended before departure. For **Ireland, Finland, Malta and Norway**, a **tapeworm (echinococcus) treatment** administered by a veterinarian is mandatory within a precise window before arrival, and recorded in the dog's travel documents (EU pet passport or health certificate).

## External parasite prevention

Fleas, ticks and mosquitoes (leishmaniasis in warm southern regions) are more prevalent in certain areas. Adapt the **external parasite protection** (spot-on, collar, chewable tablet) to the destination and the season, and check that it's current at the time of departure.

{{< alerte type="attention" >}}
Some waiting periods are **non-negotiable** (rabies vaccine, rabies titer test for strict countries — including EU re-entry from certain destinations). Plan **several weeks to several months** ahead depending on the destination.
{{< /alerte >}}
