A senior dog, or one living with a chronic illness, can absolutely travel — as long as you **adapt the pace** and plan for their needs. A little preparation avoids a lot of risk.

*This article is for information only and does not replace a veterinarian's advice.*

## The pre-trip check

See your veterinarian to confirm your dog is **fit to travel**, review current treatments and get the **prescriptions** you'll need (especially for international trips). Bring the **health record**, a list of medications and, for a long stay, a sufficient supply. Locate a **veterinarian** near your destination in advance.

## Adapting the trip

Favor **short travel legs** with **frequent breaks** to stretch, drink and relieve themselves. Upgrade the **comfort** (a soft, memory-foam bed), help them climb in (a ramp) and avoid sudden exertion. A senior dog recovers more slowly: take it easy on them.

## Watching heat and cold

**Temperature regulation** is less efficient in senior or sick dogs: be doubly vigilant in the heat (heatstroke) and in the cold. Stick strictly to the **medication** schedule despite the change in routine, and watch for any change in condition (appetite, energy, breathing).

{{< alerte type="check" >}}
Before any trip with a **senior or sick dog**, ask your **veterinarian** whether your dog is fit to travel and what precautions their specific condition requires.
