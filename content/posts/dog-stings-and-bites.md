On walks, a curious dog with its nose to the ground sometimes gets stung or bitten. Most insect stings are harmless, but some situations — a snake, a processionary caterpillar, an allergic reaction — are true emergencies.

*This article is for information only and does not replace advice from a veterinarian.*

## Insect stings (wasp, bee)

A single sting on the body causes **local swelling** and pain, usually harmless. The situation becomes **serious** if the sting is in the **mouth or throat** (risk of swelling that obstructs breathing), if there are **multiple stings**, or if there's an **allergic reaction** (swollen muzzle, hives, lethargy, difficulty breathing). Remove any visible stinger, keep the dog calm, apply cold if possible, and **get emergency care** at any serious sign.

## Snakebite (viper)

A viper bite causes **sharp pain** and **rapid swelling** (often on the muzzle or legs), sometimes with two puncture marks. **Keep the dog calm** (exertion spreads the venom), do **not apply a tourniquet**, do not cauterize, and get **to a veterinarian immediately**: this is an emergency. The same first-aid rules apply to other venomous snakes wherever you're traveling.

## Processionary caterpillar

Contact with a **pine processionary caterpillar** (springtime, pine trees — a hazard to know about in southern Europe in particular) is **very serious**: the stinging hairs cause a violent reaction, especially if the dog licks it — **swollen tongue, heavy drooling, possible tissue necrosis**. Rinse the mouth with water **without rubbing** and rush to the veterinarian **as an emergency**.

{{< alerte type="attention" >}}
A snakebite, contact with a processionary caterpillar, a sting in the mouth/throat or an allergic reaction (swollen muzzle, difficulty breathing) are **life-threatening emergencies**: head to the veterinarian **without delay**.
