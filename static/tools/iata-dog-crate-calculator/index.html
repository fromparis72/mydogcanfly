Choosing the right airline travel crate for your dog

To fly with a dog traveling in the cargo hold, an IATA-compliant travel crate is mandatory. But you still need the right size: not too small (denied at boarding, and uncomfortable), not too big (extra cost and less stability). This calculator helps you find the crate size that fits your dog, from their measurements or their breed, using the official IATA formula.

A properly sized crate is the foundation of a safe, low-stress trip: your dog must be able to stand up in it without touching the roof, turn around, and lie down naturally. Airlines will deny boarding to an animal whose crate does not meet these rules.

How do you measure your dog to find the right crate size?

The IATA method relies on four measurements of your dog, taken while standing:

  • A — Length: from the tip of the nose to the base of the tail.
  • B — Leg height: from the floor to the elbow.
  • C — Width: at the widest point of the body.
  • D — Total height: from the floor to the top of the head (ears up).

The calculator applies the official formula: crate length = A + ½B, width = C × 2, height = D. You get the minimum interior dimensions, then the matching standard size (#100 to #700), in both centimeters and inches.

IATA crate sizes, from #100 to #700

Approved travel crates come in standard sizes, from #100 (very small dogs, like a Chihuahua) to #700 (giant dogs, like a Great Dane). Weight gives you a first ballpark, but only measurements guarantee the right choice: two dogs of the same weight can need different crates. When in doubt between two sizes, pick the larger one.

Cabin or cargo hold: when is the crate required?

A small dog (up to about 8 kg / 18 lb, carrier included) usually travels in the cabin, in a soft carrier. Above that, they travel in the hold, in a rigid IATA-compliant crate. Frequently asked questions about IATA travel crates

What size crate does my dog need?

It depends on your dog's measurements, not just their weight. Enter their dimensions in the calculator to get the right approved size, with enough room for comfort.

How do I know if a crate is IATA-compliant?

A compliant crate is rigid, well ventilated on at least three sides, with a lockable metal door and a leak-proof floor. See our guide to choosing an airline-approved crate.

Should my dog get used to the crate before the trip?

Yes: introduce the crate several days ahead so your dog feels safe inside it, which greatly reduces stress on flight day.

Travel with your dog, stress-free

Choosing the right travel crate is a key step toward flying safely with your dog. Once you have the size, check the entry requirements for your destination and get your companion ready for the trip: your dog will travel more relaxed — and so will you.
