Traveling abroad with your dog: which entry requirements?

Every country sets its own entry rules for a dog. Before you travel, it's essential to check the requirements for your destination: a single missing document can lead to a denied boarding or quarantine. This checker shows, country by country, the exact steps (microchip, vaccine and rabies titer test, pet passport, certificate, permits…), the lead times to plan for and the official sources.

Whether you're heading to Europe, Asia, Africa or the Americas, requirements vary enormously: from a few days of preparation for the European Union to several months for the strictest destinations. Planning ahead is the key to a successful trip with your dog.

The essential documents for traveling with your dog

  • ISO microchip: the identification, implanted before the rabies vaccine, is the foundation of every file.
  • Rabies vaccine: up to date and valid, given after the microchip.
  • Rabies titer test: a serological antibody measurement (≥ 0.5 IU/ml), required by many non-EU countries — and to return to Europe from some destinations.
  • EU pet passport or health certificate: depending on the destination.
  • Import permit and tapeworm treatment: required in certain countries.

Lead times that vary widely by destination

The preparation time depends mostly on the rabies titer test and any waiting period. Expect about 21 days for the European Union, one to two months for many destinations, and up to 6 or 7 months for the most demanding countries (Japan, Australia, Mauritius…). Frequently asked questions about entry requirements

Does my dog need a passport to travel?

For the European Union and many countries, yes: the EU pet passport, issued by a veterinarian, records the microchip and rabies vaccination. Outside the EU, a specific health certificate is often required on top.

When should I start the process?

As soon as you know the destination. For a rabies titer test and any waiting period, allow several months for the strictest countries.

Does my dog need another titer test to return to the EU?

Coming from a non-EU country that is not rabies-favorable, a valid rabies titer test is needed to return: it must be done before you leave the EU.
